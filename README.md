## Introduction

.ve-media wc:1984_Big_Brother_poster.jpg left height=400px

During 1966 and 1976, China underwent a period known as the Cultural Revolution, also called the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution. Under the leadership of Chairman Mao Zedong, Mao believed communist ideas in China were weakening. This eventually led to the mobilization of young people known as the “Red Guards.” These youth were encouraged to remove old customs and traditions from society as Mao aimed to purge capitalist and bourgeois influences. Mao sought absolute control, and this movement created major social and political chaos across China. The Cultural Revolution lasted until Mao’s death in 1976.

This historical period connects strongly to George Orwell’s *1984*, where society is controlled by a totalitarian government led by “Big Brother.” In Orwell’s dystopian world, the government constantly watches and manipulates citizens through propaganda, censorship, and fear. Winston Smith, the novel’s protagonist, questions the Party’s control and searches for truth and freedom. Both the Cultural Revolution and *1984* demonstrate how authoritarian governments suppress individuality in order to maintain complete control over society.

## The Censorship and Fear Parallel

<param ve-image 
       label="Control Through Fear and Censorship" 
       description="Cultural Revolution propaganda poster"
       license="public domain" 
       url="https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/fcbdaa769b9af99483452e0dbeae5e1044a9a40f/32_46_1075_645/master/1075.jpg?width=700&quality=85&auto=format&fit=max&s=4d1711a3e13033b4795177eb0c53eecd">

Both *1984* and the Cultural Revolution in China demonstrate how authoritarian governments use censorship and fear to maintain control over their citizens. In *1984*, the Party utilizes censorship by forcing Winston to rewrite historical records inside the Ministry of Truth, ensuring the Party’s complete control over information. Orwell writes, “Who controls the past controls the future; who controls the present controls the past.” This quote highlights how controlling information becomes the foundation of totalitarian power.

Similarly, Mao Zedong used censorship and fear during the Cultural Revolution to eliminate political enemies and silence opposition. Through propaganda, fear, and public pressure, citizens were forced to support Mao’s authority. Both governments manipulated information and created fear among the population in order to prevent independent thought and crush resistance. This parallel reinforces how authoritarian governments maintain power through the suppression of individuality and free expression.

## Importance of Image

The Cultural Revolution propaganda poster directly supports the idea that censorship and fear were tools of authoritarian control. The Red Guards in the poster are shown carrying weapons and bayonets, representing the violence used to silence opposition and intimidate citizens. The figure holding Mao’s *Little Red Book* symbolizes how only one ideology was permitted. This reflects the censorship in *1984*, where the Party rewrites history to control what people think and believe.

Like Winston’s world, this propaganda glorified the government and pressured citizens into obedience. Both the poster and Orwell’s novel reveal how authoritarian governments use fear to crush independent thought and maintain control over society.

## The Proles of 1984 and the Citizens of China

<param ve-image 
       label="Propaganda and the People" 
       description="Mass rally during China's Cultural Revolution"
       license="public domain" 
       url="https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/f3f1a770a7e8e7f403725b8634721f1bba00a937/0_15_5100_3060/master/5100.jpg?width=700&quality=85&auto=format&fit=max&s=6742567b56574794beaaa331c3d1992e">

In *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, George Orwell portrays the proles as the largest social class in Oceania, yet also the most politically powerless. The proles are kept obedient through cheap entertainment and the neglect of education. This reflects the experiences of ordinary Chinese citizens during the Cultural Revolution, who were similarly stripped of independent thought and redirected toward the goals of the Communist Party instead of true political participation.

In both societies, the ruling governments recognized that an uneducated and disorganized lower class posed little threat to their authority. The Party in *1984* had no interest in increasing the proles’ awareness, just as Mao’s government discouraged independent political thinking. Both governments suppressed the political power of the majority population through propaganda, misinformation, and the destruction of critical thought. As a result, citizens never realized the collective power they possessed.

## Significance of Image

This photograph captures a mass rally during China’s Cultural Revolution, where citizens raise their fists in a public display of loyalty. At first glance, the image appears to show political unity and participation. However, the rally actually reflects the success of Mao’s regime in creating public performances of obedience rather than genuine freedom of expression.

The crowd mirrors Orwell’s depiction of the proles in *1984*. Although the people represented the majority of the population, their ability to challenge authority was weakened through propaganda and manipulation. Just as the Party distracted the proles with entertainment and misinformation, Mao’s government redirected citizens toward organized demonstrations that reinforced government control. The image demonstrates how authoritarian systems can manipulate the public into supporting their own oppression.

## Conclusion 
In conclusion, both 1984 and the Cultural Revolution demonstrate how governments can use censorship, propaganda, and fear to control citizens and strengthen authoritarian power. By rewriting information, silencing opposition, and discouraging independent thought, both the Party and Mao Zedong were able to manipulate society and maintain control. These examples reinforce the idea that when individuality is destroyed and truth is controlled, authoritarian governments can more easily dominate society.
